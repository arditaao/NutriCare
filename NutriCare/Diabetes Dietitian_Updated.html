Understanding Pre-Diabetes

Pre-diabetes refers to a condition where an individual's blood sugar levels are higher than normal but not high enough to be diagnosed as type 2 diabetes. This can be a confusing and concerning diagnosis, but it’s important to understand that it doesn’t mean that you are destined to develop type 2 diabetes.

  • What is Pre-Diabetes? It's when your blood sugar levels are elevated but not yet in the diabetes range. In the UK, an HbA1C test reading between 42-47mmol/mol typically indicates prediabetes.
  • Is Pre-Diabetes Inevitable? Statistics show that not everyone with prediabetes will develop type 2 diabetes. Around one-third of people with prediabetes will develop diabetes, one-third will maintain the prediabetic range, and one-third may even return to normal blood sugar levels.
  • Why is Pre-Diabetes Controversial? The term “prediabetes” implies that people are on the inevitable path to type 2 diabetes, which isn’t always the case. Many individuals can improve their blood sugar levels with proper lifestyle changes.
  • Different Conditions of Pre-Diabetes: Pre-diabetes can manifest differently, with high blood sugar either in the fasting state, after meals, or both. Unfortunately, the HbA1C test doesn’t distinguish between these variations, which means additional testing may be necessary.

Type 2 Diabetes Remission

Type 2 diabetes is characterized by two main issues: the pancreas doesn't produce enough insulin, and the body becomes resistant to the insulin it does produce. However, recent advancements in treatment have led to a shift from simply managing diabetes to achieving remission.

  • What is Remission? In the context of diabetes, remission means that a patient's blood sugar levels return to normal, and they no longer need medications to control blood sugar. This can last for years, but it’s important to monitor blood sugar levels regularly.
  • Why Not "Reversal"? We use the term "remission" rather than "reversal" because "reversal" can create false hope. While many people achieve normal blood sugar levels, some may experience a return to higher levels, requiring further management.
  • Can Remission Last? Yes, remission can be long-term, but it's essential to continue monitoring your health. Some people experience a temporary return of elevated blood sugar levels, which is why ongoing management and support are crucial.

It's important to remember that remission doesn't mean the condition is completely gone, but rather that it is under control. By continuing with healthy lifestyle choices, it’s possible to keep blood sugar levels within a normal range and avoid complications associated with diabetes.
